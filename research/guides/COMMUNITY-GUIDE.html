Grow Your Own Food

A step-by-step guide for growing fresh herbs and greens indoors — designed for everyone, including elderly family and community members

AISim Community Initiative — West Springfield, MA
01
Getting started — 5 easy steps
1
Pick a spot in your home
You need a small corner — just 2 feet wide by 4 feet long. A spare room, basement, or large closet works perfectly. The space should have an electrical outlet nearby and stay between 65–80°F.
2
Get your DWC bucket system
Deep Water Culture is the simplest system for beginners. Plants sit in small net pots filled with clay pebbles, and their roots hang in nutrient-rich water kept bubbly by an air pump — like a fish tank for plants. A 4-bucket kit costs $50–$80.
3
Set up your grow light
Plants need light to grow. The Spider Farmer SF2000 LED light ($140–$150) covers your whole setup. Hang it 18–24 inches above your plants. Set the timer to turn ON for 14 hours and OFF for 10 hours each day.
4
Mix your nutrients + check pH
Plants eat liquid nutrients instead of soil. Mix the Flora Trio (3 bottles) into your water following the label directions — about 2.5 mL of each per gallon. Then test your water's pH and adjust it to between 5.8–6.2. This is the most important number to watch.
5
Plant your seeds and watch them grow
Start with lettuce — it's the most forgiving crop and teaches you everything. Place seeds in small rockwool cubes or directly in net pots with clay pebbles. Your first harvest comes in about 30–45 days. Once you succeed with lettuce, add herbs and Caribbean greens.

04
Growth plan — start small, scale smart
Phase 1 — Months 1–2
Learn the basics ($345–$456)
Set up your 4-bucket DWC. Grow lettuce and basil to learn pH management, nutrient mixing, and harvest timing. Expect 1–2 pounds of greens per week. Start scotch bonnet seeds now — they take 3–4 months before producing fruit.
Phase 2 — Months 3–6
Add Caribbean crops (+$200–$400)
Add a second system and light. Expand to 20–40 plant sites producing 3–5 lbs/week. Grow callaloo, culantro, thyme, and green onions. Your scotch bonnet plants should begin fruiting.
Phase 3 — Months 6–12
Catering supply scale (+$500–$2,000)
Scale to 50–100+ plant sites across multiple systems. Produce 10–20 lbs/week of herbs and greens plus a steady pepper harvest. This is where production becomes meaningful for Pan Road Catering.
Phase 4 — 12+ months
Community expansion
Help family and community members set up their own systems. Create a network of indoor growers across West Springfield. Share harvests, trade knowledge, and build food independence together.

The #1 rule: Always check pH
If your water's pH is outside the 5.5–6.5 range, plants cannot eat — no matter how much nutrient you add. Test daily with your digital meter. Add nutrients FIRST, stir, then adjust pH. A few drops of pH Down at a time. This one habit prevents 90% of hydroponic problems.
06
Made for everyone — accessibility adaptations

Designed for elderly family and people with disabilities

  • Waist-height setup: Place buckets on a table or cart at 30–36 inches — no bending or kneeling required. Wheelchair accessible.
  • Rolling cart option: Put the whole system on wheels ($20–30) so it comes to you, not the other way around.
  • Smaller buckets: Use 3.5-gallon instead of 5-gallon — weighs 29 lbs full instead of 42 lbs.
  • Large-print labels: Color-code everything — green buckets for greens, red for peppers, blue for herbs.
  • Easy-grip tools: Spring-loaded pruning shears and large syringes for nutrient measuring. No tiny droppers.
  • Pre-mixed nutrients: Mix a week's worth at once in a gallon jug. Just pour when needed.
  • Simplest system (Kratky): No pump, no moving parts, no daily maintenance. Fill the jar, place the plant, check once a week. Under $5 per plant.
  • Grow buddy system: Pair each grower with a helper for monthly deep-cleans and troubleshooting. Nobody grows alone.
